Based on when I was still in college until last year: **Every student is cheating with AI**.

- When I was a computer science TA, it was clear that tons of students were using ChatGPT to do the assignments and coming to office hours only when that failed them.
- There were certain computer science classes (Web Programming) where essentially *everyone* was turning in exactly what ChatGPT gave them.
- In group projects, everyone was passing off AI writing as their own, with [varying levels of quality](/chatgpt-semester).
- In language classes, people were turning in homework and essays that were heavily *assisted* by ChatGPT.
- More and more exams were closed-book but held online, during which \~most students were using a second device to cheat in some way.

I'm not here to condemn these behaviors — in fact, depending on the statute of limitations, I may have been an offender as well.

But the strange thing is, cheating with AI (or cheating on an online exam) doesn't feel like cheating. Plagiarism or paying someone to do your work definitely feel like cheating. Most students would never do that.

But having AI write your essay, or asking for the right answer as a double-check when you're taking a real-life midterm without any proctoring in your bedroom, feels easy. *So* easy that there's no way it could be cheating. It's no big deal!

I've heard of stories where students get huge consequences for using AI to cheat. Almost everybody completely gets away with it, but the occasional student fails the course or gets placed on academic probation.

On the one hand, that's totally within the university's rights. They broke the rules.

But also, what else should you expect? If a personal trainer told you to strictly diet, but then kept leaving you alone surrounded by desserts, it'd feel kinda unfair for the trainer to kick you out of the program for indulging.

And for exams, it's *ludicrous* that an increasing number of exams seem to be held online. It's a Covid-ism that persisted, which now combines with the era of magical free machines that answer any question in seconds. 

Cheating is *wrong* — I think every student knows that. But when everyone's doing it, the failures are environmental, not individual.
